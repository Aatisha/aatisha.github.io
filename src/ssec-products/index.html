Problem

Scientists at SSEC lack a unified platform to study climate data from various satellites.

The SSEC scientists collect extensive data on snow, ice, and polar winds from various satellites and instruments in the Arctic and Antarctic regions. This data is curated and presented as images that can be generated on a daily, weekly, or monthly basis which are then closely monitored to study climate patterns. However, the current mechanism stores the images in folders and hence lacks effective navigation and flexibility for accommodating future products needed to allow for the efficient study of both recent and historical climate data and be flexible enough to accommodate future products.

How might we allow scientists to efficiently navigate, compare, and analyze snow, ice and polar winds data across different timeframes and regions.
Research

User Interviews

To better understand the needs, pain points, and current workflows of the scientists at SSEC, I conducted interviews with four scientists who regularly work with snow, ice, and polar winds data. The objective was to uncover challenges they face in accessing and analyzing satellite data, as well as to gain insights into their expectations for an improved data visualization platform.

Interview Questions

  • How do you currently access satellite data for snow, ice, or polar winds?

    The goal here was to understand the tools or processes used to gather data and their efficiency.

  • What challenges do you face when comparing data from different satellites, regions, or timeframes?

    This question helped identify pain points in their data comparison methods and the limitations of the tools they currently use.

  • What features or functionalities would make the process of exploring and analyzing climate data easier for you?

    This question was crucial in gathering their expectations for the new platform—what they wished for but didn't currently have.

  • Are there any specific tools or platforms you've used that you found particularly useful or limiting?

    This helped in benchmarking the current platform's usability against competitors like NOAA and NASA.

How it started?

The Challenge

Climate change is an urgent global challenge that demands our collective attention. As we witness its profound impacts on our planet, it's clear that action is needed at every level – from world leaders to individual citizens.

Reading articles about climate change and watching the news makes people wonder what they can do about it. Because not everyone has the same resources, opportunities, and abilities to make contributions, the cause gets forgotten and unattended. But the fact that the majority of the population uses digital platforms in their daily lives, the platform itself could be leveraged to encourage people to make simple yet effective contributions.

Initial Problem Discovery

There exists a disparity between individuals' desires to contribute to environmental conservation and the tangible actions and contributions made toward this goal.

Secondary Research Insights

The initial problem discovery led to exploring white paper research to understand the climate crisis and the dire need for everyday personal contributions, as well as to understand how UX finds its place in driving this toward an effective solution.

The major problem lies in the inability of users to pursue their motivations toward environment-friendly actions. These are mainly because of the lack of:

  • Resources

  • Opportunities

  • Awareness

  • Fun and accountability

  • Sense of achievement

  • Abilities

How Might We

The complete understanding of the situation helped me frame the "how might we" statement to narrow down my problem statement in a way that fits a vast area of the underlying problem to provide an efficient solution. It focuses on making the process of making an impact more personal and fun.

How might we make people more aware of climate change issues through a digital platform so they make more sustainable life decisions in a fun yet accountable way.

Inception of Sustain Dane Mobile App

The core idea of the design solution is to curate an application is to make people make sustainable and eco-friendly life choices through setting up individual or team goals and make the process of achieving these goals accountable and fun.



Brainstorming

Based on the research insights, I brainstormed several ideas. The ideas are showcased below:

From the various categories of brainstormed ideas ranging from plantations, food and lifestyle, achievements and metrics, and dashboard, I drilled down to three theme insights that could potentially help users facilitate greater engagement in everyday environmental efforts.

  • Collaboration and accountability drive motivation and fun: Making the entire journey accountable, fun, and feel more personal. This was done by allowing users to create individual or team sustainable goals and work on them within a deadline.

  • Create awareness: Providing environmental awareness through articles and other resources at the tip of their touch to help them make more informed lifestyle choices.

  • Creating a sense of achievement: Giving users a sense of accomplishment through goodies and offers or certificates.

Story Board

Storyboards are a great way to visualize a problem scenario with the focus on the user navigating through their user journey with a specific solution.

I utilised this tool to depict the problem inspired by a personal incident while living in Madison. This was when I experienced relatively higher temperatures in February which is usually not the case. This motivated me to contribute more towards the design solution I was trying to accomplish.

With a beginning, middle, and end, my storyboard stated the problem, a solution, and a final state.

Task Flow

From the theme insights generated after brainstorming, I created three user task flows that closely fulfill the idea of creating a sense of contribution, achievement, and awareness. The three task flows are:

Setting up individual sustainable goals

Setting up sustainable goals with a partner

Awarding users for their goal achievements

Screen Design

I sketched the first screen designs for each task flow screen. The goal was to produce flexible designs with room for changes and modifications later. The process also focussed on the completeness of the design by sketching all of the necessary elements on each screen.

System Design

The system design reflected the platform's core idea of representing minimalism and greenery. The colors and other components were refined with the various iterations of the design cycle.

Wireframes

With the system design and screen designs in place, I created the wireframes for all the required screens in the task flows using Figma. The wireframes depicted the overall looks and functionalities of the application. Finally, I annotated each screen. These annotations included relevant information and decisions about the design.

Final Mockup

The final stage consisted of designing the mockups. With multiple design iterations and feedback I worked on creating the final mockups. I focussed on curating an experience that was clear and easy to follow.

Lessons Learned

The entire journey of designing the UX for the Sustainability mobile app platform taught me to take a holistic approach to designing solutions to contribute to the climate crisis. Providing users with personalized experiences motivates them more to accomplish a goal. From a design perspective, information architecture plays a very vital role in defining the entire design journey.

If I Had More Time: For future work and enhancement, I would like to further incorporate a few features in the app:

  • A way to track the carbon footprints of each user.

  • A better visualization of the contributions through dashboards and graphs.
